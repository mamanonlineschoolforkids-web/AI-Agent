Teacher: Hello class! Today we are going to explore why things move and how we describe that movement. In science, we use the word Motion. But what does it actually mean? Motion simply means a change in position over time. An object is in motion when it moves from one place to another. Think about your walk to school this morning. You started at your front door, and you ended up here in this classroom. Because your position changed as the clock ticked, you were in motion! But how do we measure this? We need two very important words: Speed and Direction. Speed tells us how fast an object moves. It’s the distance you travel divided by the time it takes to get there. If you run, your speed is high; if you crawl, your speed is low.

But speed doesn't tell the whole story. We also need to know where the object is going. Direction describes the path an object follows. Is it moving north? Is it turning left? Is it going up or down? When we combine speed and direction, we get a new concept called Velocity. Imagine a car driving at 60 kilometers per hour. That is its speed. But if we say the car is driving at 60 kilometers per hour towards the city, that is its velocity. Now, does anyone know what causes an object to start moving? The answer is a Force—a push or a pull. Without a force, an object at rest will stay exactly where it is. Your book won't move across the desk unless you push it. And once something is moving, it usually keeps moving until another force stops it. Think about a ball rolling on the grass; it eventually stops because of a force called Friction. Friction is the "hidden" force that acts against motion when two surfaces touch. We also have Acceleration, which is when an object speeds up or slows down. When a plane takes off and goes faster and faster, it is accelerating. When a car hits the brakes at a red light, it is also a form of acceleration—just in reverse! So, to recap: Motion is a change in position. Speed is how fast you go, and Direction is where you are headed. Forces are the reason everything in our universe starts or stops. Look around the room right now. What objects are in motion? The clock hands are moving, the air from the fan is moving, even your pens are moving as you write. Motion is happening everywhere, all the time! For your homework, I want you to observe one moving object at home. Write down its speed—was it fast or slow? And write down its direction—where was it going? Tomorrow, we will talk about how gravity affects motion. Does anyone have any questions before we pack up? Great!
